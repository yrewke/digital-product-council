# Anonymous Disagreement Review

**Strongest memo and why:** Memo B provides the strongest owner-side buying analysis because it compares the proposed offer honestly with aggregators, WhatsApp, QR menus, POS ecosystems, agencies, and doing nothing. Its narrow differentiation claim is credible: an owned channel may offer first-party access and brand control, but it does not obviously beat aggregators on discovery, convenience, price, or experience, and profitable channel shift remains unproven (E-001, E-005, E-014, E-018, E-019, GAP-005).

**Strongest insight:** Across the memos, the most useful insight is that package boundaries should follow responsibility and operational burden rather than feature count. POS integration, delivery, automation, and customization create distinct setup, reliability, and support obligations, so making them explicit add-ons feels more natural and trustworthy than an all-in-one package (E-008 through E-013, E-017, E-021 through E-023).

**Weakest claim:** Memo D's claim that pickup is the strongest first workflow is operationally plausible but commercially unsupported. The supplied evidence does not show that enough restaurant owners want, will pay for, or will retain a pickup-centered standalone offer. It may be reliable yet too weak to beat WhatsApp, existing channels, or doing nothing. Usefulness without delivery or POS integration remains unresolved (A-003, E-015, E-017, GAP-005).

**Material disagreement:** Memo D centers the base offer on pickup reliability, while Memos A, B, C, and E describe a broader owned repeat-order destination and leave the minimum fulfilment mode less settled. From an owner's buying perspective, this is a major disagreement because the smallest operationally safe offer is not necessarily the smallest commercially useful offer. Resolving it requires testing whether pickup-only value is obvious enough to justify spend and staff change (A-003, E-018, GAP-005).

**Shared unsupported assumption:** All memos depend on owners accepting a standardized owned channel before deep integrations, then supplying onboarding inputs and sustaining routine administration. That assumption is only partially supported; local personalization pressure, setup burden, and usefulness without POS or delivery integration remain unresolved (A-001, A-003, E-021, E-023, GAP-006).

**What creates distrust:** The greatest distrust comes from presenting control, fee avoidance, rapid launch, or repeat demand as an economic buying case without comparable restaurant outcomes. Direct ordering may not improve customer price or experience, migration is unquantified, and onboarding/support burden is not observed (E-005, E-018, E-019, E-021, E-022, GAP-005, GAP-006).

**Missing evidence:** No memo can establish the owner's purchase threshold: comparable restaurant payback, profitable shifted-order contribution after incentives and fulfilment, willingness to pay for a pickup-only core, workload accepted by restaurant staff, or retention after launch (GAP-005). The pack also lacks evidence on contractual responsibility for failures/refunds, data portability, cancellation, and exit conditions. **Missing evidence: no cited evidence ID establishes these contractual and exit protections.**

**Proposed correction:** Treat the minimum package as unresolved rather than defaulting to pickup-only or a generic owned storefront. Test two clearly bounded variants: a pickup-centered core and an operations-connected variant offered only where POS or delivery is a demonstrated purchase requirement. Compare owner willingness to buy, direct usage, net contribution, staff burden, support incidents, and retention. Keep loyalty, QR dine-in, WhatsApp automation, and custom work separate until their specific value is demonstrated (E-006, E-015 through E-017, E-021, E-023, GAP-005, GAP-006).

**Evidence needed before synthesis:** Owner-side pilot evidence must show whether value is obvious enough to purchase, which integration boundary changes the buying decision, and whether the resulting direct orders are profitable after all restaurant costs. Contractual responsibility, data ownership/export, cancellation, and exit terms must also be defined before the offer can be considered trustworthy. **Missing evidence: these buyer-protection terms have no evidence IDs in the supplied memos.**
