# Anonymous Disagreement Review: REVIEW_03

**Anonymous mapping:** Memo A-E follow the alphabetical order of the five advisor-memo filenames.

**Strongest insight:** Memo C most clearly separates measurable value from benefits that resist quantification. Avoided marketplace fees have measurable value only for orders demonstrably shifted to the owned channel and only after subtracting direct-channel, fulfilment, incentive, and provider costs. First-party data, brand control, relationship ownership, and strategic optionality may matter, but the current evidence does not establish their incremental profit contribution (`E-005`, `E-014`, `GAP-005`, `GAP-006`). This framing correctly makes profitable repeat-order shift, rather than generic digital presence, the load-bearing restaurant payback thesis.

**Weakest claim:** Memo D's claim that pickup is the strongest first workflow is commercially plausible but not established by the cited evidence. `E-003` supports the importance of timeliness and accuracy, and `E-009` supports delivery exception risk, but neither proves that pickup produces the strongest restaurant value, adoption, retention, or provider economics. The claim also relies on unverified assumptions about reachable pickup demand, safe staging, staff queue ownership, and customer willingness to collect.

**Missing evidence:** All five memos lack observed evidence that the proposed smallest offer is useful enough without early POS or delivery integration (`A-003`). They also lack segment-specific UAE evidence for incremental direct-channel conversion, profitable channel shift, restaurant payback (`GAP-005`), and provider CAC, onboarding labor, support load, churn, cost-to-serve, and gross margin (`GAP-006`). Memos D and E additionally expose unresolved operational and contractual evidence needs: restaurant workload, fulfilment responsibility, refunds, service interruptions, data portability, cancellation, and exit terms (`E-009`, `E-011`, `E-013`, `E-021`, `E-022`).

**Major disagreement:** The central disagreement is the minimum sellable and operationally viable core. Memo D proposes pickup as the base workflow and defers delivery, table ordering, catering, and reservations. Memos A, B, C, and E support a standardized owned-channel core but leave fulfilment scope less settled and acknowledge that excluding POS and delivery integration may make the offer too weak (`A-003`, `E-008`, `E-017`). This is not merely a packaging disagreement: it determines restaurant value, operational burden, onboarding scope, support exposure, and which segments should be avoided. The memos otherwise broadly agree that premium/fine dining, weak-repeat-demand restaurants, operationally unreliable restaurants, high-customization prospects, and low-price/high-touch accounts are poor initial fits (`E-004`, `E-023`, `E-024`, `GAP-005`, `GAP-006`).

**Proposed correction:** Reframe the emerging direction as a bounded test with two unresolved hypotheses, not as a settled pickup-first package or a broadly useful standalone storefront. Hypothesis one: a standardized owned channel can capture enough profitable repeat demand while aggregators remain discovery channels (`A-002`, `E-018`, `E-019`, `GAP-005`). Hypothesis two: the smallest offer remains useful and operationally manageable without early POS or delivery integration (`A-003`, `E-008`, `E-017`, `E-021`). Synthesis should preserve pickup-first as Memo D's proposed test configuration, while withholding any claim that it is the strongest commercial core until observed pilot evidence resolves restaurant value, peak-period workload, retention, and provider cost-to-serve (`E-003`, `E-009`, `GAP-005`, `GAP-006`).
