# Anonymous Cross-Review: REVIEW_02

**Commercial question:** Which Stage 1 reasoning best identifies the operational conditions under which a direct channel remains reliable during normal and peak service?

**Verdict:** conditional

**Strongest insight:** Memo D provides the strongest operational insight: the channel is viable only when it creates one authoritative queue with an accountable shift owner and named fallback, and when the first workflow is narrow enough to test under peak load. Its distinction between normal-period manageability and peak-period failure risk exposes the main adoption burden that the other memos mostly treat as a sales-qualification or scope-control issue. Pickup is the strongest first workflow because the restaurant controls preparation and handoff; delivery adds dispatch, rider, status, and exception dependencies whose observed reliability is unresolved (`E-003`, `E-009`, `E-011`, `E-013`). Memo D also specifies the most decision-useful pilot: train the primary and fallback owners, drill exceptions, compare normal and peak periods separately, and measure staff interventions and fulfilment failures.

**Weakest claim:** Memos A, B, C, and E broadly support a standardized owned-channel core before delivery or POS integration, but they do not establish that this core reduces work rather than adding a second queue that staff must reconcile manually. The claim remains dependent on unresolved assumption `A-003`. Standardization may bound provider effort, but it does not by itself prevent stale availability, missed acceptance, duplicate entry, inaccurate ready times, or failed handoffs during peaks. Integration can add setup and support burden (`E-017`, `E-021`), yet excluding it can also create recurring restaurant labor and reliability weakness. Memo D recognizes this contradiction; the other memos underweight it.

**Missing evidence:** None of the memos has observed evidence that likely-fit restaurant staff will consistently adopt the required workflow across shifts and peaks. Missing operational evidence includes acceptance latency, availability accuracy, order and modifier accuracy, ready-time accuracy, handoff delay, failed handoffs, cancellations/refunds, exception-resolution time, manual re-entry, staff interventions per order, status bypass, training time, fallback use, and peak-versus-normal deterioration. Observed delivery SLA performance and provider support load also remain unresolved (`E-011`, `E-013`, `GAP-006`). Restaurant payback cannot be interpreted reliably until these fulfilment costs and failure effects are measured (`GAP-005`).

**Major disagreement:** The material disagreement is the minimum useful first offer. Memos A, B, C, and E describe a broadly standardized direct-ordering or owned-channel core and leave fulfilment readiness to qualification, packaging, or later add-ons. Memo D argues for a stricter pickup-first base offer with explicit capacity controls, status ownership, handoff checks, fallback procedures, and peak testing before adding outsourced delivery or other workflows. This is not merely a packaging disagreement: it changes staff training, mistake exposure, recurring discipline, support burden, and the probability that the channel damages repeat demand (`E-003`, `E-008` through `E-013`, `A-003`).

**Proposed correction:** Carry forward the standardized owned-channel direction only as a pickup-first operational pilot. Require one named shift owner and fallback, one visible queue, pre-service availability checks, capacity-aware ready-time controls, a handoff check, written failure procedures, and exception drills before launch. Keep the existing fallback channel active. Run multiple normal periods and at least two representative peak periods, with explicit stop or narrowing gates for unseen orders, stale availability, status bypass, failed handoffs, excessive manual re-entry, or material peak deterioration. Add outsourced delivery or POS/order-management integration only after the pilot shows whether each change removes more operational burden than it introduces (`E-003`, `E-008`, `E-009`, `E-017`, `E-021`, `A-003`, `GAP-006`).

**Critical assumption:** A likely-fit restaurant can sustain named shift ownership, availability discipline, status accuracy, exception handling, and handoff checks during peak service without unacceptable added labor. This is not established by the current evidence.

**Tradeoff:** A pickup-first pilot narrows initial appeal and may under-test restaurants whose demand depends on delivery. It materially improves the ability to observe adoption, mistakes, training burden, and peak reliability before additional fulfilment dependencies obscure the source of failure.

**Disagreement expected:** Memos focused on owner value, competitive differentiation, or provider repeatability may argue that pickup alone is too weak to establish demand. That concern is valid, but broadening the first pilot before staff discipline and peak reliability are observed would make failure causes and support burden harder to diagnose.

**Evidence request:** No new research. Use the bounded live pilot to collect the missing operational evidence and resolve `A-003`, `GAP-005`, and the operational portion of `GAP-006`.
