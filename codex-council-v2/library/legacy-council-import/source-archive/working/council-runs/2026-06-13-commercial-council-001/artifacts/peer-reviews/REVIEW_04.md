# Anonymous Cross-Review: Review 04

**Strongest insight:** Memo D provides the strongest decision-shaping insight: the smallest useful offer should be defined by whether it creates one authoritative workflow and reduces existing work, not merely by whether it has a narrow feature set. Its pickup-first boundary exposes a risk underplayed elsewhere: a standalone owned channel can add manual reconciliation, queue ownership, training, and peak-period failure risk when it does not replace an existing step or integrate with operational systems (`E-003`, `E-008`, `E-009`, `E-017`, `E-021`, `A-003`).

**Weakest claim:** The recurring-subscription direction appears across Memos A, B, C, D, and E, but it is not yet supported as the right commercial model. `E-025` establishes that subscription and hybrid models exist; it does not establish willingness to pay, retention, provider margin, or that the smallest standalone offer creates enough recurring value. Generic low-ARPA churn evidence and the absence of UAE provider economics make this a testable hypothesis rather than a preferred model (`E-024`, `E-025`, `GAP-006`).

**Missing evidence:** The packet lacks observed evidence that a standardized owned channel without early POS or delivery integration is useful enough to buy, operate, and retain. It also lacks restaurant-level incremental conversion, profitable channel shift, payback, peak-period workload, onboarding labor, support burden, churn, and provider gross margin (`A-003`, `GAP-005`, `GAP-006`). Memos A and E additionally identify unresolved responsibility, portability, and exit boundaries, but the other memos do not incorporate those as purchase and support risks (`E-009`, `E-014`, `E-022`).

**Major disagreement:** Memo D recommends pickup as the base workflow and treats other fulfilment modes as separate gated additions. Memos B, C, and E describe a broader repeat-order or direct-ordering core without resolving which fulfilment workflow makes it operationally useful; Memo A goes further by deferring delivery and POS integrations for provider repeatability. The disagreement is material: narrowing provider scope may preserve repeatability while making the restaurant offer too weak or too labor-intensive to adopt (`E-008`, `E-017`, `E-021`, `E-023`, `A-001`, `A-003`, `GAP-006`).

**Proposed correction:** Reframe the emerging direction as a bounded pilot hypothesis, not a settled core offer or pricing-model direction: test a standardized pickup-first owned channel at a likely-fit repeat-demand restaurant, while keeping the existing fallback channel active. Require evidence that it creates one authoritative workflow, performs acceptably during peaks, produces incremental profitable direct demand, and stays within measured onboarding and support limits before broadening the fulfilment scope or preferring subscription pricing. Compare the pilot honestly against WhatsApp, aggregators, relevant POS/order ecosystems, and doing nothing for that restaurant's actual job; treat QR menus and agencies as unlike alternatives unless the restaurant's need makes them relevant (`E-001`, `E-005`, `E-015`, `E-016`, `E-017`, `E-018`, `E-019`, `GAP-005`, `GAP-006`).
