# Advisor Memo: Market Economics Analyst

**Commercial question:** What is the strongest commercially viable restaurant direct-channel offer, including segment fit, smallest useful offer, bundle boundaries, safe promises, and pricing-model direction without final prices?  
**Verdict:** conditional  
**Strongest finding:** The clearest measurable restaurant-side value is not generic digital presence; it is the observable economics of suitable repeat orders that actually shift from fee-bearing marketplace channels to an owned channel. That direction is most plausible for repeat-heavy QSR, fast-casual, and neighborhood concepts, but `GAP-005` prevents quantifying conversion, profitable channel shift, or restaurant payback.  
**Evidence used:** E-001, E-003, E-004, E-005, E-006, E-008, E-009, E-011, E-013, E-014, E-018, E-019, E-021, E-023, E-024, E-025, GAP-005, GAP-006; assumptions A-001, A-002, and A-003.  
**Critical assumption:** A-002 is load-bearing: marketplaces can remain discovery channels while the owned channel captures enough suitable repeat demand to create incremental restaurant value. The retrieved evidence supports coexistence as an available strategy, not a proven profitable migration outcome (E-019, GAP-005).  
**Tradeoff:** A narrowly standardized offer limits onboarding, support, and customization exposure, but may omit integrations or local personalization needed to make the channel operationally useful and commercially differentiated (E-021, E-023, A-001, A-003).  
**Disagreement expected:** Other advisors may place more weight on first-party data, brand control, convenience, or rapid launch. Those benefits may matter, but the current evidence does not establish their incremental profit contribution, and direct ordering does not necessarily beat aggregator price or experience (E-014, E-018).  
**Evidence request:** Do not conduct another research round at this stage. Before economic promises, final package boundaries, or final pricing, resolve `GAP-005` with segment-specific pilot evidence and `GAP-006` with observed provider cost-to-serve and retention evidence.

## Evidence Classification

### Verified Facts

- Aggregator commissions and associated fees can compress restaurant margins, and operators object to loss of customer data and experience control. Contract terms vary, so the magnitude cannot be generalized (E-005).
- Timely delivery and order accuracy are important retention determinants in the cited UAE study, although its small young-adult convenience sample limits generalization (E-003).
- Generic SaaS evidence indicates that low-ARPA/SMB models tend to face higher churn and weaker retention than higher-ARPA models. This is boundary evidence, not proof of UAE restaurant-provider economics (E-024, GAP-006).

### Estimates

- No decision-grade estimate of incremental direct-channel conversion, restaurant payback, profitable channel shift, provider CAC, onboarding labor, support load, churn, cost-to-serve, or gross margin is available. No numeric ROI or payback estimate is supportable (`GAP-005`, `GAP-006`).

### Assumptions

- **A-002, load-bearing:** enough repeat demand can move to an owned channel while aggregators continue to provide discovery. If customers do not shift, the fee-saving and first-party relationship case weakens materially (E-018, E-019, GAP-005).
- **A-001, load-bearing:** the provider can standardize delivery across selected segments without becoming a custom-development service. The personalization-versus-scalability contradiction remains unresolved (E-021, E-023, GAP-006).
- **A-003, load-bearing for the smallest offer:** an offer excluding delivery and POS integrations remains useful enough to buy and retain. Available integration capabilities show potential operational value, but usefulness without them is unresolved (E-008, A-003).

### Inferences

- QSR, fast-casual, and neighborhood concepts are the strongest initial-fit candidates because the evidence synthesis associates them with stronger direct-channel fit and repeat-order use cases (E-004). This is a segment inference, not a measured conversion result.
- Premium/fine-dining concepts and exposed mid-market casual concepts are weaker initial fits for a standardized repeat-order proposition (E-004). This does not establish that every restaurant in those segments is unsuitable.
- The economic case is more defensible when framed as aggregator coexistence and selective repeat-demand capture than as aggregator replacement (E-005, E-018, E-019).

## Value Boundaries

### Measurable Value

- Avoided marketplace commissions and fees on orders demonstrably shifted to the owned channel can be observed restaurant by restaurant (E-005). Net value still requires subtracting channel operating, fulfilment, incentive, and provider costs; those values are absent (`GAP-005`, `GAP-006`).
- Order accuracy, delivery timeliness, and fulfilment reliability can be monitored as pilot gates because they affect retention, but retrieved provider thresholds are candidate assumptions rather than verified operating standards (E-003, E-011, E-013).
- Onboarding effort, support incidents, customization requests, and retention can be measured during pilots to test provider economics, but no UAE segment data currently validates them (E-021, E-023, GAP-006).

### Benefits That Resist Quantification

- First-party customer data, brand control, and control of the customer experience are plausible benefits, but capability evidence does not prove incremental profit (E-014).
- Aggregator independence, relationship ownership, and strategic optionality may matter to owners, but the evidence does not price those benefits.
- Loyalty capability is not equivalent to loyalty value; the evidence records a conflict between program supply and consumer use (E-006).

## Segment Direction

### Stronger-Fit Segments

- Prioritize bounded pilots with QSR, fast-casual, and neighborhood concepts that already exhibit repeat demand, have an identifiable base reachable outside aggregators, and can maintain accurate order and fulfilment workflows (E-003, E-004, E-019).
- Fit improves where the restaurant can measure marketplace fee exposure and compare shifted-order contribution after all direct-channel costs. The current evidence does not establish how often that condition produces payback (`GAP-005`).

### Poor Fits or Segments to Avoid Initially

- Avoid treating premium/fine dining as a primary repeat-order direct-channel segment without a distinct use case; current segment evidence points elsewhere (E-004).
- Avoid restaurants dependent mainly on aggregator discovery with weak evidence of reachable repeat demand, because coexistence may produce little profitable shift (E-001, E-018, E-019, GAP-005).
- Avoid operationally unreliable restaurants where order accuracy, status discipline, and fulfilment consistency cannot be maintained; channel ownership does not repair those failures (E-003, E-009, E-013).
- Avoid high-customization prospects and price-sensitive low-ARPA accounts until provider onboarding, support, churn, and gross-margin boundaries are observed (E-023, E-024, GAP-006).

## Load-Bearing Payback Assumptions

The restaurant economic case fails or remains unproven when any of the following is false:

1. Incremental or shifted repeat orders are sufficient to offset direct-channel operating costs and any customer incentives (`GAP-005`).
2. Direct ordering preserves an acceptable customer price and experience relative to aggregators (E-018).
3. Fulfilment reliability and order accuracy do not degrade retention (E-003, E-013).
4. The offer remains standardized enough that onboarding, support, and customization costs do not erase provider margin (E-021, E-023, `GAP-006`).
5. Excluding early delivery and POS integrations does not make the smallest offer too weak to buy or retain (A-003).

## Bounded Offer and Pricing-Model Direction

- Treat a standardized, coexistence-oriented owned channel for repeat demand as a **pilotable direction**, not a proven package or economic promise (E-019, `GAP-005`).
- Keep delivery, POS integration, and material customization outside the smallest standardized scope until their onboarding and support burdens are measured. Their exclusion must itself be tested because A-003 remains unresolved (E-008, E-021, E-023).
- Use pricing-model tests that expose provider workload and restaurant value separately: bounded onboarding/setup responsibility, a recurring standardized-service component, and separately scoped high-burden integrations or customization. Existing subscription and hybrid models establish available directions, not validated provider economics (E-021, E-023, E-025, `GAP-006`). This is a test direction, not a final package ladder or price.
- Do not rely on a low-price SMB subscription thesis solely to drive adoption; generic SaaS retention evidence warns of churn risk, while UAE provider economics remain unknown (E-024, `GAP-006`).
- Do not use performance, savings, conversion, or payback promises until incremental channel shift and net restaurant economics are observed (`GAP-005`). Do not finalize any pricing model until provider CAC, onboarding labor, support load, churn, and gross margin are observed by target segment (`GAP-006`).
