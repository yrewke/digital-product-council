# Advisor Memo: Business Builder

**Commercial question:** What bounded offer, acquisition, onboarding, support, customization, and pricing-model direction could a one-developer provider repeatedly sell and service without becoming a custom-development agency?

**Verdict:** conditional

**Strongest finding:** A one-developer business is repeatable only if the core offer is deliberately narrower than the full direct-ordering category. The commercially defensible direction is a standardized owned-channel product for suitable restaurants, sold alongside rather than as a replacement for aggregators, with a controlled launch process, self-service recurring administration, and separately scoped add-ons. This direction may reduce delivery and support variance, but profitability is not established because UAE CAC, onboarding labor, support load, churn, cost-to-serve, and gross margin by segment remain unavailable (`GAP-006`).

**Evidence used:**

- Acquisition should target observable fit rather than broad restaurant reach. UAE restaurant discovery is heavily digital/social, but efficient provider acquisition depends on contribution-margin discipline, and provider CAC is absent (`E-020`, `GAP-006`). The initial sales motion should therefore be founder-led and qualification-based, using a repeatable demonstration and a narrow fit checklist rather than paid scale or bespoke proposals.
- The best available segment direction is QSR/fast casual and neighborhood concepts with plausible repeat-order behavior, while premium/fine dining and exposed mid-market casual concepts appear weaker fits (`E-004`). This is an inference, not a proven acquisition cohort.
- Positioning should not promise aggregator replacement. Aggregators retain reach and visibility, while direct channels offer data/control but do not necessarily deliver a better price or experience; coexistence is available, but successful channel-shift rates are unquantified (`E-001`, `E-005`, `E-014`, `E-018`, `E-019`, `GAP-005`).
- A standardized launch is plausible, but menu and POS-related setup introduces manual mapping burden and no onboarding-time distribution is available (`E-021`). Repeatability therefore requires a fixed launch checklist, limited included data preparation, explicit customer deadlines, and an acceptance gate before launch.
- Recurring support can become commercially dangerous. Competitors condition buyers to expect multichannel 24/7 support, priority tiers, and dedicated success management, but actual ticket volume and cost are unknown (`E-022`, `GAP-006`). A one-developer provider cannot include those obligations in the standard offer.
- Local personalization demand conflicts directly with the scalability benefit of limiting customization (`E-023`). Custom requests must be classified before sale, not negotiated informally after launch.
- Low-ARPA/SMB SaaS commonly faces higher churn and weaker retention than higher-ARPA models (`E-024`). A low-priced, high-touch subscription would be structurally risky for a one-developer provider even though UAE-specific retention economics are unavailable (`GAP-006`).
- Subscription, hybrid subscription/per-order, and commission-free-positioned models exist, but none is validated as profitable for this provider (`E-025`, `GAP-006`). The safer direction is a bounded setup/onboarding charge plus recurring subscription for standardized service, with separately priced finite add-ons. Per-order participation should be deferred because it adds billing complexity, aligns provider revenue with unproven channel shift, and may invite broader operational accountability (`GAP-005`, `GAP-006`).
- Delivery and POS integrations add workflow, mapping, reliability, and SLA obligations (`E-008` through `E-013`, `E-017`). They are unsustainable as included early capabilities for one developer and cannot carry unqualified performance promises.

**Bounded commercial direction:**

| Boundary | Direction | Commercial consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Core offer | Standardized branded owned-channel presence with a fixed capability set and aggregator-coexistence positioning. | Creates a repeatable demo, proposal, launch checklist, and support surface; avoids claiming measurable channel shift or payback (`E-014`, `E-018`, `E-019`, `GAP-005`). |
| Acquisition | Founder-led outbound/referrals/partner introductions aimed at qualified restaurant archetypes; use one standard demo and fit checklist. | Keeps early CAC observable and rejects poor-fit prospects before onboarding; repeatability and profitability remain unproven (`E-004`, `E-020`, `GAP-006`). |
| Included onboarding | One fixed launch path, bounded content/menu import, one designated customer owner, one review cycle, and launch acceptance. | Contains manual setup burden exposed by `E-021`; extra cycles or cleanup cannot be silently included. |
| Self-service | Routine menu/content edits, availability updates, basic promotions, customer-data export, staff/user administration, and standard help guidance. | These recurring tasks must be customer-operated or the subscription becomes an unbounded managed service. Evidence supports the need to limit customization but does not prove restaurants will successfully self-serve (`E-023`, `GAP-006`). |
| Standard support | Documented business-hours support, defined response targets rather than resolution promises, and a bounded incident/escalation policy. | Avoids commercially unsustainable 24/7 and dedicated-success expectations (`E-022`); support cost remains unresolved (`GAP-006`). |
| Separately priced add-ons | Additional launch/data cleanup, extra training, additional locations, approved template-level personalization, managed content changes, and other finite work with explicit scope and acceptance. | Converts foreseeable labor variance into visible commercial scope without turning the core subscription into custom service (`E-021`, `E-023`). No add-on price is supportable yet. |
| Deferred | POS integrations, delivery integrations, advanced loyalty, per-order pricing, complex migrations, and any capability requiring proven operational SLAs or channel-shift economics. | These may be valuable, but introduce manual mapping, reliability, support, and economic exposure not validated for one-developer delivery (`E-006`, `E-008` through `E-013`, `E-017`, `GAP-005`, `GAP-006`). |
| Rejected | Bespoke websites/features, unlimited revisions, restaurant-specific workflows, 24/7 human support, dedicated success management, guaranteed delivery performance, guaranteed ROI/channel shift, and open-ended managed operations. | These create uncapped labor, reliability, or promise risk that conflicts with standardization and lacks evidence support (`E-013`, `E-022`, `E-023`, `GAP-005`, `GAP-006`). |

**Critical assumption:** Restaurants with plausible direct-channel fit will accept a standardized product, complete their required onboarding inputs, and self-manage routine recurring tasks without demanding the local personalization, integrations, or support intensity that would turn the provider into a custom agency. This assumption is only partially supported (`A-001`, `E-021` through `E-025`) and must be tested through measured pilots.

**Tradeoff:** Narrow scope reduces sales appeal to restaurants seeking a fully managed, integrated, or highly personalized solution. That loss is commercially preferable to winning customers whose onboarding and recurring burden cannot be served predictably by one developer. The model should optimize for qualified-fit retention and bounded cost-to-serve, not maximum close rate.

**Disagreement expected:** Other advisors may argue that delivery, POS integration, loyalty, or more local personalization are necessary to create enough restaurant value. The Business Builder objection is not that these capabilities lack value; it is that including them before measuring onboarding labor, support incidents, SLA exposure, retention, and gross margin would make the one-developer business commercially unbounded (`E-008` through `E-013`, `E-017`, `E-023`, `GAP-006`). There may also be disagreement that a useful early offer can exclude delivery and POS integrations because `A-003` remains unresolved.

**Evidence request:** Do not conduct further research inside Stage 1. Before classifying the model as profitable or setting prices, run a bounded pilot and record per-account acquisition time/cost, sales-cycle length, onboarding labor by task, number of revision cycles, customer completion delays, support incidents and minutes, recurring manual tasks, custom requests and disposition, churn/retention signals, infrastructure/vendor cost, and contribution margin. Segment the results by restaurant type and package. This is the minimum evidence needed to resolve `GAP-006`; channel conversion, payback, and migration outcomes must separately resolve `GAP-005` before economic promises or per-order pricing are considered.
