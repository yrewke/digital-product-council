# Anonymous Disagreement Review

## Strongest Insight

Memo A provides the strongest commercially complete insight: repeatability depends less on adding capabilities than on enforcing responsibility boundaries before sale. Its connection between qualification-led acquisition, fixed onboarding, customer-operated recurring administration, bounded support, separately priced finite work, and rejection of open-ended obligations directly addresses how a one-developer provider avoids becoming a custom agency. This is consistent with the observed onboarding and personalization tensions and the absence of provider unit economics (`E-021`, `E-022`, `E-023`, `GAP-006`).

## Weakest Claim

The weakest shared claim is that routine menu/content edits, availability, promotions, and administration can simply become self-service while the offer remains useful and retainable. The memos correctly identify these tasks as commercially dangerous recurring burden, but they do not establish that target restaurants have the discipline, capacity, or willingness to perform them reliably. Memo D explicitly identifies the need for an accountable shift owner and warns that recurring discipline is the main reliability burden; Memo E identifies hidden workload as a purchase blocker. Therefore, self-service is a required commercial boundary, not yet a validated customer behavior (`E-003`, `E-014`, `E-021`, `GAP-006`).

## Missing Evidence

- Acquisition: qualified lead volume, sales-cycle effort, close rate, and CAC by restaurant archetype are absent (`E-004`, `E-020`, `GAP-006`).
- Onboarding: provider labor, restaurant labor, completion delays, revision cycles, and menu/data cleanup variance are absent (`E-021`, `GAP-006`).
- Support and recurring burden: ticket frequency, support minutes, recurring manual interventions, escalation causes, and business-hours support acceptance are absent (`E-022`, `GAP-006`).
- Retention: no evidence shows whether a standardized non-integrated core remains useful enough to retain restaurants or whether low-ARPA churn overwhelms the model (`E-024`, `A-003`, `GAP-006`).
- Custom-request risk: local personalization pressure is established, but the frequency, close-rate impact, rejection impact, and delivery burden of custom requests are unknown (`E-023`, `A-001`, `GAP-006`).
- Restaurant value: profitable channel shift, payback, and incremental direct demand remain unresolved, limiting both acquisition claims and retention expectations (`E-018`, `E-019`, `GAP-005`).
- Commercial controls: contractual responsibility, data portability, cancellation, migration, and exit expectations are not established.

## Major Disagreement

The material disagreement is the minimum commercially viable boundary. Memos A and C favor deferring POS, delivery, and other integration-heavy obligations to protect standardization and provider margin. Memo D argues that a standalone channel may add reconciliation and peak-period work unless it replaces a manual step or creates an authoritative queue. Memos B and E preserve the same concern: usefulness without early POS or delivery integration remains unresolved (`E-008`, `E-017`, `E-021`, `A-003`).

This is not primarily a capability dispute. Commercially, it determines acquisition fit, onboarding variance, support load, retention, and custom-request pressure. A core that is too narrow may be inexpensive to deliver but difficult to sell and retain; an integrated core may sell more easily but create unbounded recurring obligations.

## Proposed Correction

Treat the offer as a bounded commercial pilot with explicit disposition rules rather than as a package ready for broad sale:

- **Must be self-service:** routine menu/content changes, availability, basic promotions, customer-data export, and staff administration. Admission requires a named restaurant owner for these tasks. Measure completion failures and provider interventions before treating self-service as viable (`E-014`, `E-021`, `GAP-006`).
- **Separately priced:** finite data cleanup, extra onboarding or training, additional review cycles, extra locations, managed content changes, and approved template-level personalization. Each requires fixed scope and acceptance criteria (`E-021`, `E-023`).
- **Deferred:** POS integration, delivery integration, advanced loyalty, per-order pricing, and complex migrations until pilots show that demand and retention justify their onboarding, support, and reliability burden (`E-006`, `E-008` through `E-013`, `E-017`, `GAP-005`, `GAP-006`).
- **Rejected:** bespoke workflows, unlimited revisions, informal custom requests, 24/7 human support, dedicated success management, open-ended managed operations, and guaranteed ROI, channel shift, or fulfilment outcomes (`E-011`, `E-013`, `E-022`, `E-023`, `GAP-005`, `GAP-006`).

Before synthesis advances the direction, require a pilot scorecard that records acquisition effort, onboarding labor and delays, self-service failures, support minutes, recurring provider interventions, custom requests and dispositions, restaurant usage, retention signals, and contribution margin by account. The decision gate is whether the standardized core can be sold and retained without recurring provider work exceeding its explicit commercial boundary (`A-001`, `A-003`, `GAP-006`).
